Turning AI Hype Into Business Results

The gap between AI interest and AI outcomes is significant — and widening. Here's what separates the organisations actually capturing AI value from those still running pilot programmes two years in.

GreyRadius Consulting · May 2026 · 7 min read

Most leadership teams are not sceptical about AI. The scepticism, where it exists, is about whether their organisation can actually capture value from it — or whether AI investment will follow the pattern of previous technology waves: significant spend, impressive demos, and modest operational impact.

That concern is well-founded. The gap between organisations that are generating real commercial value from AI and those still in pilot mode has widened considerably over the past 18 months. And the difference isn't primarily about technology — it's about how organisations approach the question of where and how AI fits into how they compete.

The problem with most AI initiatives

Most enterprise AI initiatives start with technology rather than problem. The question is "what can we do with AI?" rather than "what problem, if solved, would meaningfully change our commercial or operational performance?" Starting with technology leads to a proliferation of interesting experiments, most of which don't scale — because they were never connected to a business outcome that leadership cares about enough to invest in transforming.

The second structural problem is pilot management. Organisations run pilots to de-risk AI investment. But pilots succeed or fail based on whether the conditions are right for scaling — and most pilot designs don't define those conditions in advance. A pilot that "worked" but has no clear path to production is not de-risking investment; it is delaying the real decision while consuming resources.

Third: AI initiatives often underestimate the organisational change required. Technology integration is the smallest part of an AI deployment. The larger challenge is changing how people work — which use cases they trust the AI to handle, where they intervene, how quality is managed, how the economics of human and AI work are rebalanced. Organisations that treat AI as a technology project tend to get stuck at this point.

What organisations capturing AI value do differently

The pattern across organisations that are generating measurable commercial value from AI is consistent across three dimensions.

They start with a high-value problem, not a technology

Rather than asking what AI can do, they identify the two or three business problems where a solution would generate the most value — measured in revenue, cost, speed, or competitive advantage. They then assess whether AI is actually the best solution to that problem. Sometimes it is. Sometimes a simpler automation approach, a process redesign, or a human capability investment is more effective. The question is the problem, not the technology.

They design pilots for scale from the start

Effective AI pilots are designed with an explicit definition of what success looks like and what conditions would need to be true for the solution to scale. Before the pilot begins, the questions are already answered: Who owns this in production? What does the integration into existing systems look like? How will quality be managed? What is the commercial model for scaling? A pilot designed this way produces genuine learning. A pilot designed to "test the technology" produces a demo.

They invest in the human side of the transition

The organisations achieving the clearest AI results have typically invested as much in the change management — redesigning workflows, retraining people, establishing new accountability structures — as in the technology. This is unglamorous compared to the technology deployment, and it is why most AI programmes underdeliver.

The three questions worth asking

If you are running or considering an AI initiative, three questions determine whether it is on the right track:

  • Can you quantify what success looks like — in business terms, not technology metrics? If not, the initiative isn't connected to the right problem.
  • Is there a defined path from pilot to production — with ownership, integration design, and a commercial model already decided? If not, the pilot is a proof of concept without a plan for what comes next.
  • Is the organisational change programme funded and resourced separately from the technology investment? If not, the technology will likely outpace the organisation's ability to use it.

The organisations that answer yes to all three are the ones producing results. The ones still in pilot mode, two years in, have typically answered no to at least one.
