What Does a Management Consultant Actually Do? (And When Do You Need One?)

The honest answer — what consulting work actually involves, when it genuinely adds value, and what to look for when choosing a firm. No jargon.

Supra Sankar · April 2026 · 6 min read

Most people who haven't worked closely with consultants have a fuzzy picture of what they actually do. The stereotype involves expensive slide decks and generic recommendations that disappear into a drawer. The reality, when the work is done well, is considerably more useful — and considerably more specific.

Here is the honest version of what management consultants do, when you genuinely need one, and what separates the engagements that produce real impact from the ones that don't.

What management consultants actually do

At the core, consultants do three things that most in-house teams struggle to do simultaneously: structured diagnosis, cross-sector pattern recognition, and independent analysis on a compressed timeline.

Structured diagnosis. Most organisations have good instincts about what is wrong but lack the structured process for turning those instincts into a clear, evidence-based problem statement. Good consulting work starts with a rigorous diagnosis of the actual problem — not the presenting symptom. This sounds straightforward. In practice, the presenting problem (slow growth, declining margin, a failed market entry) is often a symptom of something more fundamental: a misaligned go-to-market, a product that hasn't found its segment, a cost structure that doesn't reflect how work actually flows.

Pattern recognition. A consultant who has worked across 20 market entry mandates or 30 operational improvement projects carries pattern recognition that no single organisation can develop through its own experience. They know which approaches have worked in comparable situations, which assumptions tend to be wrong, and where organisations typically go wrong at each stage of a given type of decision. This is especially valuable when an organisation is doing something for the first time — entering a new market, launching a new product category, building a new capability.

Speed and independent perspective. Internal teams have competing priorities, approval cycles, and institutional constraints that slow down analytical work. External consultants can move faster. And because they are not embedded in internal politics, they can surface conclusions that would be difficult to deliver from inside the organisation.

When you genuinely need one

The cases where external consulting adds the most value tend to share three characteristics: the decision is high-stakes and non-recurring; the in-house team lacks specific knowledge or bandwidth; and the cost of error is high relative to the cost of the engagement.

Specific trigger situations where the value is clearest:

  • Market entry or expansion. Entering a new geography or category requires primary research, competitive analysis, regulatory navigation, and execution planning that most organisations haven't done before in that specific context.
  • Fundraising. Structuring a pitch and building investor materials requires understanding of what investors are actually looking for — which is different from what the company believes is its most compelling story.
  • Go-to-market strategy. When growth has stalled and it isn't immediately obvious why — whether it's ICP definition, positioning, channel selection, or sales process — a structured external diagnosis is often faster and more accurate than internal iteration.
  • Major restructuring or transformation. When the decision involves significant organisational change, independent perspective on the right approach has value both analytically and politically.

What good consulting looks like versus what to avoid

Good consulting engagements are specific about what will be delivered, how, and by when. They involve primary research — actual conversations with customers, competitors, and market participants — rather than synthesis of publicly available data. They produce recommendations that are specific enough to be acted on, not frameworks that require further elaboration before they can be implemented.

What to avoid: engagements that produce generic deliverables with no primary research underpinning; firms that apply the same analytical framework to every client regardless of context; consultants who recommend against the client's instincts without the evidence to back it up; and any engagement where the outputs are unclear at the outset.

The most useful question to ask before engaging any consultant: "What will we know or be able to do at the end of this engagement that we can't do today?" If the answer isn't specific, the engagement probably isn't structured correctly.
