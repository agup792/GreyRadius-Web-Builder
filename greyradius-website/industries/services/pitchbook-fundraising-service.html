Pitchbook and fundraising support

Raising capital for emerging market expansion requires a pitch book that investors in those markets can believe. Most pitch books recycling analyst reports and desk research fail with sophisticated investors who know those markets – because the numbers do not match what those investors see on the ground. GreyRadius builds pitch books grounded in primary research: 30+ expert interviews with buyers, regulators, competitors, and distributors in the target market. When a Gulf investor asks about your addressable market in Saudi Arabia, the answer comes from our primary research in Riyadh – not an analyst forecast.

Why now? Fundraising markets for India, Gulf, and Africa expansion are active but increasingly competitive – India-focused funds, Gulf sovereign wealth capital, and Africa-focused DFIs are all deploying, but expect rigorous primary research to justify market size claims. The pitch books that succeed are those where the market data can withstand scrutiny from investors who know the market personally.

Market Intelligence

What the data says.

Primary research-backed pitch books close at significantly higher rates with emerging market investors – investors who know India, Gulf, and Africa immediately identify desk research and discount it.

Market size validation from primary research produces more conservative but more credible numbers – a $500M addressable market validated by 30+ buyer interviews is more fundable than a $2B estimate from an analyst report.

Investor-grade pitch books for emerging market expansion cover six elements beyond market size – regulatory pathway, competitive intelligence, distribution economics, team credibility, financial model, and comparable exits.

Series A and B rounds for emerging market expansion typically require 6–8 months of investor engagement – the pitch book must be built to withstand sustained investor due diligence across multiple rounds of questions.

Market Reality

What makes this market hard.

  • Most pitch books for emerging market expansion rely on secondary data – analyst reports and desk research that sophisticated investors immediately identify as unverifiable and discount accordingly.
  • Market size claims are routinely inflated – total addressable market figures from analyst reports include segments that international companies cannot access creating investor credibility gaps.
  • Financial models for emerging market expansion are often built without primary cost data – distribution margins, regulatory compliance costs, and talent costs in India, Gulf, and Africa differ significantly from headquarters assumptions.
  • Comparable exits and investor references for emerging markets are harder to identify – but are critical for sophisticated investors making unfamiliar market bets.
