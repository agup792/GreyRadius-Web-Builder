Gulf logistics market entry strategy

The Gulf is one of the world's most significant logistics hubs – with Jebel Ali the world's ninth-busiest container port, Dubai International Airport the world's busiest for international freight, Saudi Arabia's NEOM logistics city and Vision 2030 infrastructure investment, and Oman's Duqm port creating new logistics infrastructure at scale. International 3PL operators, cold chain logistics companies, freight forwarding businesses, and logistics technology providers are all evaluating Gulf market entry as GCC trade volumes grow and logistics infrastructure expands. GreyRadius helps logistics companies validate Gulf demand, navigate free zone and regulatory requirements, identify partners, and execute entry.

Why now? Gulf logistics investment is at an all-time high – Saudi Arabia's National Transport and Logistics Strategy, UAE's National Logistics Strategy, and Oman's port development programmes are all creating procurement opportunities for logistics service and technology companies. GCC e-commerce growth at 25% annually is driving demand for last-mile and fulfilment logistics infrastructure across UAE and Saudi Arabia.

Market Intelligence

What the data says.

Gulf logistics market is projected to reach $100B by 2028 – driven by e-commerce growth, NEOM construction logistics, and regional trade hub development across GCC.

Jebel Ali free zone handles 33% of UAE's non-oil GDP and serves as the logistics hub for the entire Middle East, Africa, and South Asia region creating significant throughput demand.

Saudi Arabia's NEOM logistics city will be the world's largest purpose-built logistics infrastructure – creating significant procurement for logistics technology, cold chain, and 3PL services.

Gulf cold chain logistics is growing at 15% annually – pharmaceutical distribution, food imports, and fresh produce logistics all driving significant cold chain infrastructure demand.

Market Reality

What makes this market hard.

  • Free zone versus mainland logistics operation has significant implications – free zone logistics operators serve imports and exports efficiently but mainland distribution requires a separate UAE LLC structure.
  • ICV and Saudisation requirements apply to logistics operations – minimum local employment thresholds affect staffing cost structures for logistics operators in Saudi Arabia significantly.
  • Customs and trade documentation requirements are complex across GCC markets – each country has different clearance procedures and documentation standards requiring specialist expertise.
  • Competition from established global logistics operators – DHL, Agility, Aramex, and regional operators – is intense across all logistics service categories in Gulf markets.
